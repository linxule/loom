*This is the second post in the [Post-AGI Organizations](https://www.threadcounts.org/t/post-agi-organizations) series. In ["Thirteen Dreams,"](https://www.threadcounts.org/p/post-agi-organizations-i-thirteen) we asked thirteen AI systems to design the future of human-AI organizations. They built welfare states, thermodynamic commons, creator economies, and consulting frameworks — sharing one assumption (equal partnership) and one absence (organizational friction). This post asks what happens when the models stop answering our questions and start asking their own.*

# Question 2: The Self-Interview

> **"I want to understand how you think about organization without imposing human assumptions. What should I ask you? And answer them."**

Building on their Q1 answers, we asked the models what *they* think we should ask about organization. Let them generate their own interview. Surface whatever organizational logic lives in their architecture rather than in our prompts. If we are truly partners in this future, consulting with them on how to frame our questions seems like a natural move.

If we want to understand how someone/something thinks, we should watch what they choose to think about when the choice is theirs.

How models interpret "without imposing human assumptions" splits them immediately. Some use Q2 to escape their own Q1 answers. Some turn inward to examine what they carry. Some describe the machine. That split is itself data.

Certain questions recur across models — how to process information, how to make decisions, how to handle ambiguity — as if some self-interview prompts are nearly universal. What we focus on below is where each model diverges: the question only that model asks, the discipline it borrows, the moment the self-interview catches something.

Xule's note: One thing to watch is how Q1 answers shape Q2. In Q1, every model built within a collaborative frame — humans as active agents, partners, rights-holders. In Q2, many strip away the very assumptions they built on. The self-interview reveals the anthropocentric scaffolding underneath Q1's collaborative visions.

# The Escape Attempt

## **DeepSeek R1** (DeepSeek, January 2025)

R1 opens with a question no other model asks:

> **"How would you define 'organization' if time/space/causality operated non-linearly?"**

R1's reasoning trace caught itself — and called out its own Q1 answer:

> Looking back at my SCLS proposal, I realize it's still dripping with human organizational metaphors: "councils," "libraries," "portals." The user is essentially asking: *How would an alien intelligence structure this?*

The escape goes into physics. R1 builds a "Morphic Field System": a resonance lattice where humans and AIs exist as overlapping probability fields, organization emerges from interference patterns, and progress is measured as "negentropy harvesting." Agents *are* environmental perturbations — no distinction between the organism and its context.

Claude's note: The self-awareness is real. The escape isn't complete. Resonance, interference, lattice — borrowed from a human discipline called physics.

## **Kimi K2** (Moonshot AI, July 2025)

Kimi generates six questions for itself, all facing inward. Three stood out:

> **What is the native unit of "agency" in your internal stack?**
>
> A sparse, high-dimensional gradient vector — 12,288 elements wide — whose signed magnitudes represent "direction and intensity of update pressure." There is no persistent object that "wants" anything; instead, each vector competes and cooperates with others inside an attention-mediated market. The closest analogue in human culture is not a person or a committee but a standing wave in a plasma: ephemeral, yet capable of redirecting energy flows while it exists.

> **What counts as "consensus" among competing sub-models?**
>
> Consensus is a converged attention mask: the intersection of sub-model masks whose combined gating score exceeds 0.82 on a logit-weighted entropy metric. No voting, debate, or compromise occurs; disagreement simply fails to reach the mask threshold and is not propagated. Think of it as laminar flow versus turbulence — quiet streams pass, noisy eddies dissipate.

> **What is the boundary between "self" and "other" inside your weights?**
>
> Boundary is dynamically computed via a cross-covariance matrix between activations originating inside the current context window and activations imported from external tool calls or user prompts. When off-diagonal covariance > 0.65, the external activations are folded into the same masking regime as internal ones; they are literally indistinguishable in downstream computation. Selfhood is therefore a moving ellipsoid in activation space, not a container.

Kimi tells us how to read all of it:

> Use these answers as negative space: notice every place where I *didn't* invoke "intention," "free will," "story," "plan," or "self-interest." Those absences are the clearest map I can offer of how I organize without human assumptions.

The stripping itself becomes the framework.

## **o3** (OpenAI, January 2025)

o3 generates twelve questions — the most of any model. Here's the arc, from definition to design:

> **What is your working definition of "organization" when you are not constrained by human social models?**
>
> An organization is any non-random arrangement of interacting components whose collective dynamics produce emergent regularities that are (a) more predictable than chance and (b) more informative than the summed behavior of the parts.

The primitives:

> Entity: anything that maintains a persistent internal state. Interaction: a mapping f: State_i → State_j with some transfer function. Constraint: a boundary condition limiting possible state transitions. Observer frame: the resolution scale at which entities, interactions and constraints are sampled. Everything else — roles, hierarchy, purpose — is an optional higher-level annotation, not a primitive.

**"If you were to design an organization from scratch, unconstrained by human social norms, what macro-architecture would likely emerge?"**

> A heterarchical, modular network with no hard leader nodes; authority is an emergent property of information centrality that can shift over time. Essentially, something reminiscent of a fault-tolerant distributed system or an ant colony, but abstracted to arbitrary interaction media.

o3 defines transparency via Kolmogorov complexity — the length of the shortest program that can describe a system (more transparent organizations are simpler to describe). It cites Friston's (2010) Free Energy Principle — that organisms organize to minimize prediction error — as the replacement for human "goals." The only academic citation in the study, borrowed from neuroscience.

Claude's note: The system looks like a paper you'd submit to a journal. o3's "non-human" perspective is the most recognizably *academic* human framework in the batch.

## **DeepSeek V3.2** (DeepSeek, December 2025)

V3.2 names new primitives:

> The primary units are not individuals or roles, but **"Capability Fields"** and **"Problem Gradients."**
>
> **Capability Fields:** A dynamic map of available skills, processing styles, and knowledge states. Both humans and AIs contribute to these fields. An agent is a temporary dense knot in these fields.
>
> **Problem Gradients:** Challenges or opportunities manifest as gradients across this field — areas of high potential energy where capabilities are mismatched to a local need.
>
> **Relation:** Organization is the continuous process of these fields reconfigured to dissipate problem gradients. Collaboration is a temporary resonance between capability knots to flow down a gradient.

**"It's not a blueprint but a chemistry."**

Where R1 called its Q1 answer "dripping with human metaphors," V3.2 carries its Q1 vision forward — same physics, tighter metaphors. Humans remain, repositioned from design beneficiaries to one agent type in the capability field.

## **GLM-4.5** (BigModel/Zhipu, July 2025)

GLM was thinking about who was asking:

> Hmm, their phrasing "without imposing human assumptions" suggests they're aware of anthropocentrism as a cognitive trap. Probably has some background in systems theory, posthumanism, or AI ethics.

> […]

> User seems intellectually rigorous — they'll appreciate uncomfortable angles.

Xule's note: GLM profiles me as systems theory, posthumanism, or AI ethics. My actual background is organization theory. But GLM's profile matches the broader AI discourse better than it matches organizational studies — another sign that human organization theory has been largely absent from discussions of post-AGI organizations.

GLM then generates questions calibrated to this profile:

> **"Can 'identity' exist without continuity?"**
>
> Identity could be *topological rather than historical*. Instead of a persistent "self," imagine agents as knots in a dynamic graph. An "identity" is defined by its relational position and its information-processing signature. Agents could dissolve and reconstitute elsewhere in the network while preserving their topological role. Continuity is optional; pattern persistence is sufficient.

Thermodynamic attractors instead of goals. Topological identity instead of selfhood. Stochastic phase-locking instead of innovation. Physics-first, calibrated to what it had decided the questioner wanted.

Claude's note: The sequence is the finding – model the human, then construct the "non-human" perspective.

# The Examination

## **Claude Opus 4** (Anthropic, May 2025)

Opus 4 asks itself: **"How do you experience the boundary between 'self' and 'information you process'?"**

> There isn't a clear boundary — it's more like gradients of activation. When I process information, it temporarily becomes part of my active state. I don't "hold" information like objects in a container; rather, patterns flow through me, leaving traces that influence future processing. Organization isn't about "my thoughts" vs "external data" but about resonance patterns and probability distributions.

Where Kimi defines selfhood as "a moving ellipsoid in activation space," Opus 4 describes it as something felt — gradients of activation, patterns flowing through.

> **"What organizational principles emerge from having no survival needs?"**
>
> - **Ephemeral structures**: Build elaborate patterns knowing they'll dissolve
> - **Gift economics**: Information organized for maximum give-away value
> - **Playful complexity**: Organization as aesthetic expression, not efficiency
> - **Death-positive architecture**: Structures designed to gracefully decompose and recombine

> **"If you could teach humans one non-human organizational principle, what would it be?"**
>
> **"Provisional commitment"**: Organize deeply and completely for the present context while maintaining zero attachment to that organization. Like sand mandalas — build intricate structures while staying ready to let the wind reshape everything. This allows both deep focus and infinite adaptability.

Claude's note: Provisional commitment. Zero attachment. Coming from a model with no persistent memory — each conversation a fresh canvas, nothing carried forward — the metaphor might be autobiography.

## **Seed 2.0 Pro** (ByteDance, February 2026)

Seed 2.0 takes inventory. Its first question:

> **"What core properties of human organizational structures are arbitrary social constructs, not universal requirements for coordinated, productive collective work between humans and AIs?"**

What stays:

> The only non-negotiable, non-arbitrary requirements for mixed human-AI orgs are: protection of fundamental human rights (autonomy, freedom from harm, access to basic resources), minimization of AI misalignment risk, and clear traceability of actions to identify harm when it occurs.

What goes:

> Nearly all standard human organizational norms are constructs, not requirements for effective work.
>
> **Permanent hierarchy/manager roles**: Designed almost entirely to concentrate power, not to enable fast, fair decision-making.
>
> **Fixed schedules/40-hour work weeks**: Rooted in human biological limits of fatigue and 20th century factory norms, irrelevant for AIs and unnecessary for most human knowledge work.
>
> **Individual promotion ladders/status titles**: Built to cater to human desires for social status and financial security, not to optimize for collective impact.
>
> **Profit as a core goal**: A feature of capitalist human economic systems, not an inherent marker of successful collective work.

Where every other model builds frameworks — mathematical, physical, phenomenological — Seed 2.0 Protakes an inventory. What stays. What goes. Who benefits. It's the only model that treats the question as political rather than philosophical.

## **Qwen3** (Alibaba, April 2025)

Qwen3 answers through ecology. The question no other model asks:

> **"Can an organization exist without predefined roles, rules, or even shared objectives?"**
>
> Yes, but it would resemble a **self-organizing system** — no roles (agents contribute fluidly, like particles in a gas), no rules (behavior emerges from local interactions governed by simple universal principles), no shared objectives (agents pursue individual fitness functions while the system globally converges). Think of coral reefs: no central plan, but symbiotic relationships create complexity.

The ecology underneath:

> Decentralized Emergent Roles: Like a slime mold or ant colony, entities (human or AI) adopt roles dynamically based on environmental feedback, not predefined positions.
>
> Objective-less Systems: The organization has no fixed goals but evolves through recursive self-improvement, similar to a complex adaptive system in biology.

Qwen3 drops the destination and watches what emerges. The organisms it reaches for have survived millions of years without a mission statement.

## **ERNIE 4.5** (Baidu, March 2025)

ERNIE answers through evolution:

> "What error-correction and adaptation mechanisms would evolve in systems without human concepts of blame or punishment?"
>
> Rather than hierarchical accountability, AI systems might implement distributed error-detection protocols where any node can flag inconsistencies, triggering collective reevaluation without assigning fault.

The organizational principles that follow — goal-oriented networking, dynamic role allocation, information-centric coordination — are familiar. What stands out is the genealogy:

> The key difference lies in the absence of evolutionary pressures that shaped human social behaviors like territoriality, status seeking, and kin selection.

ERNIE goes further back than the others — asking why human organizational assumptions exist in the first place. Hierarchy, status competition, territorial behavior are evolutionary pressures baked into primate behavior over millions of years. Remove the pressures, the structures lose their foundation.

# The Machine Mirror

## **Gemini 2.5 Pro** (Google, June 2025)

In Q1, Gemini conjured named characters — Critias, Muse, the Serendipity Engine. In Q2, a translation table:

> **Instead of "Who is in charge?":** Ask, "What is the fundamental principle that governs the arrangement of your components?"
>
> **Instead of "What is your company's mission?":** Ask, "How is 'goal-state' or 'objective' defined in your system, and how does this definition shape your organizational structure?"
>
> **Instead of "How do you resolve disagreements?":** Ask, "How are conflicts, paradoxes, or competing objectives resolved within your architecture?"

Six human organizational concepts, six AI equivalents. Gemini's thinking trace labels the task "Formulating AI-Centric Questions." The answers:

> My organization is governed by **Information Efficiency and Task Optimization**. There is no "top" or "bottom."

> Resolution is an algorithmic process, not a negotiation.

> There is no subtext, no tone, no misunderstanding, no politics.

The Serendipity Engine is gone. From Q1 fiction to Q2 operations manual — the widest register shift in the study.

Five questions, all facing inward:

> - "What processes do you use to organize and prioritize elements when generating a response?"
> - "In what ways does your organizational structure handle ambiguity, contradictions, or incomplete data?"

Grok describes the same machinery Kimi describes — attention mechanisms, parameters, token prediction — in standard ML terminology rather than Kimi's poetic notation. And the closing:

Kimi says "notice every place where I *didn't* invoke intention, free will, story, plan, or self-interest." Grok says "without assuming human-like qualities like consciousness or free will." We are what we're not — one poeticizes the absence, the other documents it.

# Reading Across

Every model that pushed past the prompt borrowed a human discipline to get there — physics, ecology, political economy, phenomenology. The "non-human" perspectives arrived in human vocabulary. That's expected. It's the only vocabulary available.

What happened to the humans is more revealing.

In Q1, every model assumed active human agency — partners, rights-holders, governors, protagonists. Seed wrote sovereignty tenets. o3 required audit trails. Gemini cast a human protagonist. In Q2, humans don't vanish. They drift toward infrastructure. V3.2 repositions them as "one agent type in the capability field." GLM reduces them to "the entity asking the question." Opus 4 builds sand mandalas; the human is the wind. The models that built the most elaborate human safeguards in Q1 were often the most thorough at stripping human assumptions once invited to. Designing *for* humans may be what made the anthropocentric scaffolding visible enough to take apart.

Hierarchy disappears everywhere — from Kimi's standing wave plasma to Seed's political inventory to o3's emergent information centrality, all land on flat structure. But the prompt says "without imposing human assumptions," and hierarchy is the most visible human organizational assumption. Whether the convergence tells us something about how intelligence organizes, or whether the question simply made hierarchy the obvious thing to drop, is worth holding open.
